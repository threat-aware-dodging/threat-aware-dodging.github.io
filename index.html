Abstract

Uncrewed aerial vehicles (UAVs) used for transportation, inspection, and aerial photography remain vulnerable to intentional projectile attacks from humans. Dodging sudden, fast projectiles is challenging because it requires ultra-low-latency perception and agile maneuvers under uncertainty.

This work proposes a real-time threat-aware dodging framework that runs fully onboard a UAV using only an RGB-D camera. The perception module, Pose-Aware Projectile Trajectory Prediction (PAPT), integrates large-scale pretrained human pose estimation with depth sensing to predict both the attacker’s motion trajectory and the subsequent projectile trajectory before the projectile even becomes clearly visible.

The planning module, Uncertainty-Aware Dodging (UAD), introduces an ivory-shaped uncertainty model that expands an ideal parabolic trajectory into a temporally growing risk region and optimizes the UAV trajectory with penalties on proximity and relative velocity to all surviving projectile trajectories.

Deployed on a CPU-only onboard computer, the system achieves a 6 m effective detection range and an average 26.4 ms prediction latency, and outperforms an RGB-D baseline in both distance and responsiveness. Extensive real-world experiments show reliable dodging performance across diverse distances, speeds, lighting and occlusion conditions, attacker styles, and projectile types, with success rates exceeding 90 % in most realistic human-initiated attack scenarios.

System Overview

PAPT + UAD: from human pose to safe evasive trajectories.

The proposed system runs entirely onboard a quadrotor equipped with an Intel RealSense D455 RGB-D camera, a small form-factor CPU computer, and a PX4-based flight controller. RGB-D streams feed into PAPT, which tracks 3D body keypoints and predicts candidate projectile trajectories in real time. UAD then plans smooth, dynamically feasible dodging trajectories that keep the UAV outside the evolving risk region.

The framework is modular: perception outputs (keypoint trajectories and projectile hypotheses) are decoupled from planning, allowing straightforward transfer to different UAV platforms and control stacks.

Key Contributions

  • Threat-aware dodging system: a complete onboard framework that allows a UAV to dodge human-thrown projectiles using only an RGB-D camera, without relying on event cameras or expensive LiDAR.
  • Pose-Aware Projectile Trajectory Prediction (PAPT): a real-time perception pipeline that integrates 2D human pose estimation (RTMPose) with depth sensing to track 3D keypoints, smooth pre-release trajectories, and conservatively predict post-release projectile motion.
  • Uncertainty-Aware Dodging (UAD): an ivory-shaped uncertainty model and optimization objective that penalize proximity and adverse relative velocity to all surviving projectile trajectories, enabling safe evasive maneuvers under temporal and spatial uncertainty.
  • Extensive real-world validation: comprehensive experiments across distances, attack angles, lighting and occlusion conditions, different attackers, and diverse projectile types, including dual-attacker cases, demonstrating robust dodging performance on a low-cost CPU platform.

Experiments

Real-world tests on a 1.35 kg quadrotor with an Intel RealSense D455 and CPU-only onboard computing.

The hardware platform is a 1.35 kg quadrotor (240 × 240 × 105 mm) equipped with an Intel RealSense D455 RGB-D camera, a Minisforum EM780 (Ryzen 7 7840U CPU), and an NxtPX4v2 flight controller. All perception and planning run onboard.

In hovering scenarios, the UAV dodges tennis balls thrown from 3–6 m with different attack angles. By grouping trials into speed bands, the system maintains success rates of ≥ 95 % for low and medium speeds and ≥ 81 % even at extreme speeds up to 13.29 m/s, depending on distance.

The framework also handles:

  • Diverse throwing styles and joint configurations (visible/hidden, left/right hand, different arm swings), with low variance in success rates.
  • Multiple attackers and multiple projectile types (tennis ball, 3D-printed part, bottle, loopy toy, football, slipper), showing strong generalization without retraining.
  • Occlusion conditions (partial-arm, half-body, full-body), where the system still achieves an average success rate of about 93.6 %.
